Feelings come fast, like sun or rain,

Joy that lifts, or hidden pain.

They move inside, a busy flow,

Sometimes mixed up, down below.

We build up walls, to keep things in,

Hide the bad, pretend to win.

In quiet tears, that softly flow.

It's strong to feel, and let it be,

Name the feeling, wild and free.

Breathe it in, the good and bad,

Know what you feel, don't be sad.

For when we feel, we learn and grow,

Letting feelings come and go.

So let them come, and let them pass,

True strength is feeling, in the glass.

Title: Human Acts vs. Acts of Humans

This article explains the difference between human acts (actions done with knowledge and free will, like helping or lying) and acts of humans (involuntary actions like sneezing or blinking). Human acts are morally accountable because they are intentional. Acts of humans, on the other hand, happen naturally and are not subject to moral judgment. Understanding the distinction is important in ethics and philosophy, especially when discussing responsibility and moral decision-making.

#10 | Group 5

Title: The global landscape of AI ethics guidelines

Abstract In the past five years, private companies, research institutions and public sector organizations have issued principles and guidelines for ethical artificial intelligence (AI). However, despite an apparent agreement that AI should be ‘ethical’, there is debate about both what constitutes ‘ethical AI’ and which ethical requirements, technical standards and best practices are needed for its realization. To investigate whether a global agreement on these questions is emerging, we mapped and analysed the current corpus of principles and guidelines on ethical AI. Our results reveal a global convergence emerging around five ethical principles (transparency, justice and fairness, non-maleficence, responsibility and privacy), with substantive divergence in relation to how these principles are interpreted, why they are deemed important, what issue, domain or actors they pertain to, and how they should be implemented. Our findings highlight the importance of integrating guideline-development efforts with substantive ethical analysis and adequate implementation strategies.

Source: Anna Jobin, Marcello lenca & Effy Vayena Nature Machine Intelligence 1, 389-399 (2019)

Title: Applying Virtue Ethics to Contemporary Moral Issues: A Character-Based Approach to Modern Dilemmas

Abstract This paper explores the relevance and application of virtue ethics in addressing contemporary moral issues. Drawing from Aristotelian principles and modern interpretations, the study examines how virtues such as compassion, justice, and integrity can guide ethical behavior in the domains of bioethics, professional conduct, familial relationships, and public policy. The character-centered focus of virtue ethics offers a unique and flexible framework for moral reasoning in a complex and pluralistic society.

Introduction Contemporary moral issues, ranging from medical ethics to social justice, present challenges that often transcend rigid rule-based or outcome-focused ethical systems. Virtue ethics, with its emphasis on character and moral development, provides a nuanced approach to these challenges. This paper examines how cultivating virtues can inform ethical responses in modern contexts, particularly when traditional frameworks fall short.

Foundations of Virtue Ethics Virtue ethics originates from Aristotle's Nicomachean Ethics, which posits that the goal of human life is eudaimonia, or flourishing, achieved through the practice of moral virtues. Unlike deontology or consequentialism, virtue ethics emphasizes being rather than doing. Modern virtue ethicists, such as Alasdair MacIntyre and Rosalind Hursthouse, have expanded on these ideas, advocating for a return to character-based moral reasoning in contemporary discourse.

Application in Bioethics In healthcare, virtue ethics emphasizes the importance of traits like compassion, humility, and trustworthiness. Physicians and caregivers are encouraged to act not only according to protocol but also with empathy and moral insight. In end-of-life care, for instance, virtues guide difficult decisions with respect for patient dignity and emotional nuance.

Application in Professional Conduct Professional ethics often involve complex dilemmas where rules may be ambiguous or conflicting. Virtue ethics promotes integrity, honesty, and responsibility as guiding principles for ethical decision-making. In business, for example, a virtuous leader would prioritize fairness and accountability over mere profitability.

Application in Family and Personal Relationships Virtue ethics recognizes the central role of personal relationships in moral development. Traits like loyalty, patience, and forgiveness are essential for resolving conflicts and fostering healthy dynamics within families. Unlike impersonal ethical theories, virtue ethics appreciates the emotional and relational contexts of moral action.

Application in Public Policy and Social Justice Social and political issues such as poverty, discrimination, and environmental degradation benefit from a virtue-oriented approach. Civic virtues like justice, courage, and empathy can inspire more humane and inclusive policies. Virtue ethics encourages individuals and institutions to cultivate moral character in pursuit of the common good.

Conclusion Virtue ethics offers a compelling alternative to rule-based and consequence-oriented moral theories. By focusing on moral character and the cultivation of virtues, it provides a context-sensitive and human-centered framework for navigating today’s ethical complexities. As society grapples with multifaceted moral challenges, virtue ethics invites a renewed commitment to character, community, and moral excellence.
